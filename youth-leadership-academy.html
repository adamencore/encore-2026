Section 1

Welcome to Leadership

You're not just a leader — you're a mentor.

Congratulations on stepping into a leadership role at Encore. Just a few years ago, you were in their shoes. You know what inspired you, what frustrated you, and what made you fall in love with theater. Use that knowledge.

Your mission this week

  • Create a safe, encouraging environment
  • Teach theatrical skills with patience and clarity
  • Build a cohesive ensemble in just one week
  • Produce a 30-minute performance that showcases growth
  • Have fun while maintaining professionalism

Core values for youth leaders

  • Patience — Young Artists learn at different speeds. Celebrate small victories.
  • Clarity — Simple, clear instructions beat complex explanations.
  • Encouragement — Positive reinforcement builds confidence faster than criticism.
  • Energy — Your enthusiasm is contagious. Bring it every single day.
  • Flexibility — Things won't go exactly as planned. Adapt and problem-solve.
Section 2

Working with Ages 7–12

Understanding your Young Artists.

Ages 7–8

  • Short attention spans (10–15 min max on one task)
  • Need movement breaks frequently
  • Respond well to games and imaginative play
  • May need help with choreography repetition
  • Thrive on enthusiastic praise

Ages 9–10

  • Building independence but still need guidance
  • Can handle more complex choreography
  • Starting to understand character work
  • May compare themselves to others
  • Need clear expectations and structure

Ages 11–12

  • Capable of sophisticated performance
  • May feel self-conscious or "too cool"
  • Can be critical of themselves and others
  • Ready for leadership within the group (helpers, mentors to younger artists)
  • Need respect and age-appropriate challenges

Universal strategies

  • Keep it moving — plan transitions between activities
  • Make it fun — learning should feel like play, not work
  • Be specific — "stand here" beats "move over there"
  • Use names — learn them fast and use them constantly (make it a goal!)
  • Celebrate effort — not just talent
Red flags to report to adult leadership

A Young Artist seems injured or unwell · behavioral issues you can't manage · a Young Artist seems unusually withdrawn or upset · any safety concern · parent conflicts or questions beyond your scope. You're not expected to handle everything alone — ask for help.

Section 3

Pre-Camp Planning

Over-planning beats under-planning — but don't make more work than necessary.

Before you meet as a leadership team

  • Listen to / watch the source material multiple times
  • Identify approximately 10 songs that tell a story
  • Consider which songs need solos vs. ensemble
  • Think about basic staging possibilities

Production meeting checklist

Work through these together — tap to check them off as you go.

Creating your script

Since you're doing song showcases (not full licensed shows):

  • Brief narration or introduction between songs (no dialogue from actual shows)
  • Lyric sheets for all songs
  • Notes on staging / blocking for each number
  • Character assignment chart
  • Prop and costume list
Keep it simple

A song showcase means you're telling a story through music — no licensed dialogue required. Clear beats clever.

Section 4

Audition Day (Day 1)

Everyone gets a part.

One purpose

Your auditions serve one purpose: determining solos and groupings. Every Young Artist who shows up gets cast.

Sample structure — 3-hour session

0:00–0:15Welcome & warm-up — introduce your team, explain how the week works, lead a fun vocal + physical warm-up, set expectations: "we're all going to work hard and have fun together."
0:15–1:45Auditions — call Young Artists in small groups (4–6), teach a short 16-bar section, have them perform it back. Note confidence, pitch, stage presence, energy. Others do a supervised activity while they wait.
1:45–2:30Group activities & team building — theater games that build trust and energy; get everyone moving and laughing; start building ensemble unity immediately.
2:30–3:00Parent pickup & team huddle — thank families, remind them to check email tonight; leadership team finalizes casting.

Casting considerations

  • Balance experience across song groupings
  • Pair confident Young Artists with shy ones
  • Give everyone at least one spotlight moment
  • Consider who will mentor younger or newer artists
  • Remember: growth matters more than perfection
Evening — send the casting email

Include role assignment, what to bring tomorrow, the rehearsal schedule, and encouragement.

Section 5

Daily Rehearsal Structure

Sample daily schedule (Days 2–6) — adjust timing to your camp hours. This is a template.

0:00–0:15Arrival & warm-up — vocal warm-ups (Music Director), physical warm-ups (Choreographer), an energy-building game or song.
0:15–1:00Music block — teach new songs or refine learned ones; break into small groups by part if needed; focus on pitch, blend, dynamics.
1:00–1:45Choreography block — teach new choreography or clean learned numbers; run sections with music; polish spacing and formations.
1:45–2:00Break — bathroom, water, snack; give them actual downtime.
2:00–2:45Blocking / staging block — Director leads transitions, entrances/exits; add acting and character work; integrate music + movement.
2:45–3:15Run-through & notes — run what you've learned; give specific, positive notes; preview tomorrow's goals.
3:15–3:30Closing circle — celebrate the day's wins, remind about tomorrow, send them home excited.

Progression across the week

Day 3Continue teaching, start integrating elements
Day 4Finish teaching, run sections together
Day 5Polish and clean, run full show
Day 6Final rehearsal, tech elements, full run
Day 7Performance!
Section 6 · The Four Roles

Music Director Essentials

Teach songs with clarity, patience, and musicality. Make singing fun and achievable for all skill levels.

Teaching a new song

1

Let them hear it first

  • Play the recording or perform it yourself
  • Let them absorb the melody and energy
2

Break it down

  • Teach phrase by phrase (4–8 bars at a time)
  • Sing it, have them repeat
  • Use hand motions or movement to reinforce rhythm
3

Add lyrics

  • Once melody is solid, focus on words
  • Clarify pronunciation
  • Discuss what the song means
4

Layer in musicality

  • Dynamics (loud / soft)
  • Expression and emotion
  • Blend and listening to each other

Warm-up ideas

  • Lip trills and sirens
  • "Mee-may-mah-mo-moo" scales
  • Tongue twisters
  • Call-and-response rhythm games

Different skill levels

  • Strong singers — harmonies or leadership roles helping others
  • Developing singers — keep on melody, give encouragement
  • Shy singers — pair with confident artists, build trust gradually
?Quick fixes▾
  • Pitchy singing — slow it down, isolate the problem phrase
  • Low energy — model the energy you want to see
  • Rushing — clap the rhythm without singing, then add voice
  • Can't hear words — exaggerate consonants
Remember

You're not producing a recording. You're building confidence and joy.

Section 7 · The Four Roles

Choreographer Essentials

Create movement that's age-appropriate, exciting, and achievable. Make dance accessible for all bodies and abilities.

Keep it simple

  • Repetition is your friend
  • 8-count phrases work well
  • Big, clear movements beat intricate details

Make it visual

  • Use levels (high, medium, low)
  • Create interesting stage pictures
  • Think about formations (lines, circles, diagonals)

Build progressively

  • Teach without music first
  • Add music at half-speed
  • Gradually increase tempo

Include everyone

  • Modify for different abilities
  • Create moments for less experienced dancers to shine
  • No one sits out

Teaching choreography, step by step

1

Demonstrate full-out

  • Show the whole combo with energy
  • Let them see what they're working toward
2

Break into counts

  • Teach 8 counts at a time
  • Face the same direction they face (mirror yourself)
  • Count out loud, repeat multiple times
3

Add music

  • Start slow, drill until comfortable, speed up gradually
4

Clean and polish

  • Adjust spacing, sharpen movements
  • Facial expressions and performance quality

Formation ideas

  • Lines (horizontal, diagonal, vertical) · circles · scattered / organic groupings
  • Two groups alternating · soloists in front, ensemble behind · entrances from different areas
?Quick fixes▾
  • They're lost — slow down, repeat more
  • Timing is off — remove music, clap it out
  • Low energy — model huge performance energy
  • Spacing is messy — mark spots with tape

Director Essentials

Shape the overall vision, create compelling staging, and guide character development. You're the artistic leader.

Blocking basics

What is blocking? Where actors stand, when they move, and how they use the stage to tell the story.

  • Use the full stage — don't clump everyone center
  • Create focus — draw the eye where you want it
  • Motivate movement — give actors reasons to move
  • Think in pictures — every moment should look intentional

Directing young performers

Don't say"Move over there"
Do say"Take three steps to center stage, then turn to face stage right"
Vague context"Just look happy"
Give the why"You're excited because you just discovered something amazing" — help them understand the reason behind actions
  • Use demonstrations — show them what you want; let confident artists demonstrate; walk through it with them
  • Adjust for age — younger artists: simple, clear blocking; older artists: more nuanced character work

Find ways to make each scene have movement and an interesting layout.

Have as many Young Artists on stage as possible.

Running rehearsal

  • Start and end strong — begin with energy and focus; end with encouragement and clarity about next time
  • Manage time — know what you need to accomplish, move efficiently, don't dwell too long on one moment
  • Give notes wisely — positive first, then constructive; be specific ("let's make that entrance bigger," not "that was bad"); fix the biggest issues first
  • Keep it fun — celebrate breakthroughs, use theater games between intensive work, laugh with them

Stage Manager Essentials

You're the organizational backbone. Track everything, keep the team on schedule, ensure smooth operations.

Before camp

  • Help create the rehearsal schedule
  • Organize all materials (scripts, lyric sheets)
  • Prepare audition forms and tracking systems

During camp

  • Keep rehearsal on schedule (time warnings)
  • Track blocking and choreography notes
  • Maintain attendance
  • Coordinate logistics with adult leadership
  • Manage prop and costume tracking
  • Communicate with families

During performance

  • Call places and standby
  • Coordinate backstage traffic
  • Problem-solve in real time
  • Keep calm when chaos happens

Your stage manager kit

  • Clipboard & paper · pens / pencils / highlighters
  • Script with blocking notes · cast contact list · daily schedule
  • Tape (spike marks, quick fixes) · safety pins · tissues · band-aids

Digital tools

  • Email access for family communication
  • Phone for emergencies
  • Shared document for notes / updates

Blocking notation

Learn standard notation to track what the Director creates:

Example: "Sarah X DC on 'hello'" = Sarah crosses to downstage center when she says "hello."

✎Time management & problem-solving▾

Give warnings

  • "We have 5 minutes left in this section"
  • "We're moving to choreography in 2 minutes"

Keep the Director on track

  • Gentle reminders: "we wanted to finish this song by 2:00"
  • Suggest: "should we move on or spend more time here?" — support decisions, don't undermine

Common situations

  • Someone's missing — check with adult leadership immediately; adjust staging for rehearsal
  • Running behind — identify essential vs. nice-to-have; prioritize key moments
  • Conflict between Young Artists — redirect to the task; get adult support if needed; keep it professional
Section 10 · Leading Well

Communication & Leadership Skills

Leading without being bossy.

Authority vs. authoritarianism

Authority = confident, clear, respectful leadership. Authoritarianism = demanding, rigid, dismissive. You want authority.

Be clear

  • Say exactly what you need
  • Use simple language
  • Confirm understanding

Be kind

  • Respect goes both ways
  • Use "please" and "thank you"
  • Acknowledge effort

Be consistent

  • Follow through on what you say
  • Maintain expectations
  • Build trust through reliability

Positive language

Instead of"Don't run around"
Try"Let's save our energy for the stage"
Instead of"That was wrong"
Try"Let's try it this way"
Instead of"You're being too loud"
Try"I need everyone's eyes on me and voices off"
Instead of"Stop goofing off"
Try"Let's refocus — we've got a lot to accomplish"

Working with your leadership team

  • Respect each other's roles — don't step on toes, defer to expertise
  • Support decisions publicly, discuss concerns privately
  • Communicate constantly with quick check-ins; stay aligned on priorities
  • Problem-solve together and decide collectively when possible

When to get adult help

  • Safety concerns or injuries
  • Behavioral issues beyond your management
  • Parent complaints or questions
  • Scheduling conflicts
  • Any situation that feels over your head
Remember

You're leaders, not professionals. Asking for help is a sign of maturity, not weakness.

Section 11 · Leading Well

Behavior Management

Day 1 is crucial — establish your culture from minute one.

Key expectations

  • Respect everyone (leaders, peers, space)
  • Listen when someone is speaking
  • Give your best effort
  • Have fun while staying focused
  • Ask questions when you're confused

How to communicate them

  • State them clearly at the start
  • Model them yourself
  • Reinforce when you see them
  • Redirect when they're not met

Proactive strategies

  • Keep them busy — idle time = behavior issues; plan transitions carefully
  • Use movement — if energy is high, channel it into physical activity before expecting stillness
  • Give ownership — assign small responsibilities (line leader, prop manager); they behave better when invested
  • Catch them doing good — name and praise positive behavior immediately

Redirecting behavior — escalate gently

1

Non-verbal cue

  • Eye contact · proximity (move closer) · a gentle touch on the shoulder
2

Private quiet reminder

  • Pull them aside briefly: "I need you to focus right now" — give a chance to self-correct
3

Clear direct instruction

  • "I need you sitting criss-cross, eyes on me" — calm, firm, not angry; wait for compliance
4

Consequence

  • Short break sitting out (1–2 min) · loss of a privilege · call adult leadership for backup
!Common issues & solutions▾
  • Constant talking — "voices off, eyes on me. If I see it, we can move on." Wait for silence before proceeding.
  • Goofing off — redirect the energy: "save that for the stage!" Give a specific task immediately.
  • Exclusion / cliques — rotate partners frequently, assign mixed groupings, address directly: "we're all on the same team."
  • Frustration / giving up — break the task into smaller pieces, celebrate small progress, pair with a confident helper.
Remember

Most behavior issues stem from boredom, confusion, or lack of confidence. Address the root cause.

Section 12 · The Performance

Performance Week Prep

The days leading up to the show.

Day 5 · Polish & finalize

  • Run the full show beginning to end
  • Give detailed notes
  • Clean spacing, timing, transitions
  • Finalize costume / prop needs

Day 6 · Technical rehearsal

  • Add tech elements (lights, sound cues)
  • Practice entrances and exits with flow
  • Full dress rehearsal if possible
  • Build confidence with successful runs

Pre-performance checklist

Day 7 · Pre-show pep talk

Gather your cast in a circle, keep it short and heartfelt: "You've worked incredibly hard this week. Today isn't about being perfect — it's about sharing the joy we've created. The audience is already on your side. Let's give them a great show!" Then build energy together — hold hands, squeeze around the circle, cheer, and send them out confident.

Performance Day

Stage Manager's performance duties.

60 min beforePre-show — check all props, costumes, set pieces; confirm cast attendance; test audio / tech; walk through backstage traffic flow.
30 min beforeGather — cast in designated space; final costume / makeup checks; bathroom breaks; warm-ups (vocal, physical, energy).
15 min beforeFocus — pep talk from leadership; final reminders (smile, project, have fun); quiet focus time.
5 min beforePlaces — "places for the top of the show, please"; final check: everyone ready?
DuringRun the show — stay calm; track any blocking / prop changes; be ready to problem-solve; cue entrances if necessary.
AfterCelebrate — gather the cast for a closing circle; celebrate together; thank-yous and goodbyes.
!Handling performance issues▾
  • Someone forgets lyrics — they keep going, others support; the audience won't know
  • Someone misses an entrance — Stage Manager quietly redirects; adjust on the fly
  • Prop breaks — quick fix or cut it, keep moving
  • Tech fails — performers continue without; adapt and commit

General rule: keep going. The show must go on — solve problems without stopping.

Section 14 · You

Self-Care & Reflection

Leading is exhausting. You're giving energy all week — don't burn out.

During camp

  • Stay hydrated
  • Eat actual food
  • Get sleep every night
  • Take breaks when you can
  • Lean on your team

Red flags you're overextending

  • Snapping at Young Artists or teammates
  • Feeling resentful or frustrated constantly
  • Physical exhaustion
  • Not enjoying the process

If you're struggling

  • Talk to adult leadership immediately
  • Ask your team for support
  • Adjust expectations if needed
  • Remember: it's okay to not be perfect

Post-camp reflection

After the show, take time to think about what you learned:

  • What went really well?
  • What would I do differently next time?
  • What surprised me about this experience?
  • How did I grow as a leader?
  • What did I learn about myself?
Growth mindset

You will make mistakes — that's how you learn. You'll have moments of doubt — that's normal. You'll face challenges you didn't expect — that's leadership. The fact that you're doing this at all is remarkable. Give yourself credit.

Section 15 · You

Final Thoughts

You're building more than a show.

In one week, you're not just teaching songs and choreography. You're showing Young Artists that theater is a safe, joyful place; modeling what leadership looks like; building confidence and self-expression; creating memories they'll carry forever; and inspiring the next generation of artists.

Some of those 7–12 year-olds will be in your shoes in a few years because of you. You were once a Young Artist who needed someone to believe in you. Now you're that person for someone else.

Leadership principles to remember

  • Patience wins — they learn at their own pace
  • Clarity matters — simple, direct communication gets results
  • Encouragement builds — celebrate effort, not just talent
  • Energy is contagious — bring enthusiasm every day
  • Flexibility saves the day — adapt and problem-solve
  • Teamwork makes it work — lean on your fellow leaders
  • Kindness first — respect everyone: artists, families, your team
  • Ask for help — you're not expected to know everything
  • Reflect and grow — every camp makes you better
  • Have fun — if you're not enjoying it, they won't either
You've got this

You were chosen for this role because someone saw leadership potential in you. Trust that. Trust yourself. Trust your team. Trust the process. The work you're about to do matters. Go create something beautiful.

The Ten Agreements

How we show up — every day

The commitments every Encore youth leader makes.

1

Talk to the Young Artists with respect. We don't yell, snap, use condescending language or tone, or belittle anyone.

2

Refer to the youth as Young Artists, not kids.

3

Encore's primary goal is emotional intelligence; secondary to that is giving Young Artists a professional theater experience.

4

Young Artists are seen and heard on and off stage. Light them, make sure mics work — and make sure we stop and listen to their concerns and watch for signs of problems and challenges.

5

Safety is more important than showmanship. No stunt or choreography is more important than a Young Artist's emotional or physical well-being.

6

Effective communication is crucial. Simply mentioning important information during a production meeting is not the same as clearly and thoroughly communicating it.

7

Respect each other's expertise — stay in your lane. The costume person doesn't need to offer dance advice, and the choreographer doesn't need to comment on the music director's assessment.

8

Maintain a positive atmosphere around the Young Artists, parents, and patrons. To discuss a Young Artist, a performance, or a stressful situation, relocate to a different area for that conversation.

9

Immaturity and inexperience is a feature of the youth we work with, not a weakness. Part of our mission is to help them grow through experience and mentorship — they aren't expected to show up day one with it all figured out.

10

Set expectations up front. If we want Young Artists at rehearsal ten minutes early, or off phones during rehearsal, we have to communicate that clearly. It's not fair to assume things and hold them to it.

Building the next generation of Young Artists — and the leaders who mentor them.
