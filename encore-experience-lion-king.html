The Encore Experience

The Lion King Camp

A six-day musical theater intensive where Young Artists don't just perform a show — they learn the craft of theater-making, then put it straight to work building a Lion King concert revue.

Program Overview

Not a camp that ends in a show — a front-row education

The Encore Experience transforms the traditional theater camp into a comprehensive arts-education program. Each day opens with a hands-on Experience Session teaching a fundamental skill of theater-making — then Young Artists put it straight to work in that day's rehearsal.

The Lion King is among the most culturally and physically rich shows in the repertoire. The curriculum honors that depth — African musical tradition, animal movement as physical theater, the circle as a philosophy — while keeping the work joyful, grounded, and accessible for every age in the room.

The "learn it, use it" model

  1. Warm up and build community as a company.
  2. Learn a core theater skill in the Experience Session.
  3. Apply that exact skill in the rehearsal that follows.
  4. Watch it become part of the show you're building — day by day.

Show DNA

Built on the most fundamental theatrical idea: the circle

Themes & messages

  • You are part of something larger than yourself — a circle, a community, a legacy.
  • Responsibility isn't a burden; it's what connects you to those before and after you.
  • Running from who you are only delays the return — and the return is always possible.
  • Community — the pride, the herd, the circle — is what makes us strong.

The emotional journey

  • Beginning — the world in its fullness; everything in its place, belonging announced.
  • Middle — disruption, loss, running, forgetting — and the call back.
  • End — the return, responsibility claimed, the circle completed and begun again.

Signature element — the circle

The Circle of Life is not just a song; it's the show's entire structure, its visual language, its emotional philosophy. Our revue begins with the circle, breaks it, and restores it. Every song is an expression of the circle, a departure from it, or a return to it. The ensemble's job is to be the circle — to make the audience feel what it means to belong to something that was here before them and will continue after.

Cultural foundation

The Lion King draws from Zulu, Swahili, and the broader musical traditions of sub-Saharan Africa. This isn't decoration — it's the show's DNA. Young Artists learn Swahili words and their meanings, explore African drumming as a theatrical tool, and approach animal movement as physical research rooted in real observation and respect.

The visual world

Epic and grounded at once — vast landscapes, but every story personal. Movement-forward, earth-toned costuming with vivid accents, where animal physicality lives in the body rather than the costume.

Specialty Workshops

Three deep dives into the world of the show

Woven through the camp — not one-off add-ons, but foundations the whole revue is built on.

Day 1

The Circle of Life — African Music, Rhythm & Community

A deep dive into the African musical traditions at the show's foundation: drumming as communication, call-and-response as community structure, rhythm as a physical and collective experience.

Watch: djembe hand-drumming basics — building the heartbeat of the Pride Lands.
Connects to → ensemble identity, the circle, rhythmic precision
Day 3

Animal Movement — Physical Theater & the Natural World

Young Artists study real animals — their weight, gait, stillness, and attention — and translate that observation into physical-theater vocabulary. Not costume or mimicry: physical research.

Watch: an animal-movement warm-up — finding each creature's quality in the body.
Connects to → choreography, character physicality, the visual world
Day 4

Swahili, Story & the Meaning Behind the Music

Exploring the Swahili and Zulu in the score — the opening chant, character names, key phrases — and connecting language to meaning. Understanding what you're singing changes how you sing it.

Watch: learning Swahili with Akili and Me.
Watch: the language and story behind the music.
Connects to → vocal performance, cultural respect, thematic depth

What Young Artists Learn

Skills that outlast the show

Theater skills

  • The circle as a theatrical and staging concept
  • Animal movement — physical research and translation
  • Rhythmic precision and ensemble unity
  • Call-and-response as theatrical and communal practice
  • Physical theater — levels, weight, animal quality
  • Vocal range across the show's full spectrum
  • Concert-revue structure and thematic arc
  • Mixed-age ensemble leadership and collaboration

21st-century skills

  • Cultural curiosity and respect
  • Responsibility — carrying something for a community
  • Belonging — the lived experience of the circle
  • Persistence through difficulty, and the practice of return
  • Collaboration — the pride as a model for ensemble work
  • Empathy through character and cultural exploration

Cultural & thematic connections

  • Sub-Saharan African musical traditions (Zulu choral, South & East African)
  • Swahili language and East African cultural context
  • African drumming tradition as community practice
  • The Broadway Lion King's physical-theater tradition
  • The Circle of Life as a philosophical concept
  • Responsibility as an expression of love

Why the 7–13 range is the whole point

The mixed-age circle isn't a logistical compromise — it is the show's central image. A circle of Young Artists ranging from 7 to 13 looks and feels like a real community in a way a same-age group simply cannot. Everyone is in every number. No one steps outside the circle.

Built-in differentiation runs through every session: younger Artists (7–10) work in concrete, physical, imaginative modes; older Artists (11–13) engage in analytical, leadership, and emotionally nuanced work.

Younger Artists (7–10)

Simba-spirit roles, high-energy ensemble features, and the uncomplicated joy the early songs require.

Older Artists (11–13)

Lead solos, narration, and the show's weightier emotional moments — grief, courage, and the return.

Behind the Scenes

What makes it possible

  • The Lion King original Broadway cast recording (Lebo M., Hans Zimmer, Elton John)
  • The Lion King film soundtrack for comparison and listening
  • Drumming materials — hand drums, djembes, or improvised percussion
  • Earth-toned costume elements with vivid accent pieces
  • Fabric pieces for movement exploration
  • Design supplies in the Pride Lands palette
  • Scripts with narration and transitions
  • Color lighting — the sunrise and the shadow are tech moments
  • Reference images: the savanna, the Broadway physical-theater approach, real animals
  • A Swahili / Zulu language reference sheet for every Young Artist on Day 1

They learn what the Circle of Life actually means

Not just as a song, but as a truth: that they are part of something that was here before them and will continue after; that the people who came before live in them; that the community around them holds them; and that returning to who they are is always possible, and always worth the journey.

Every Young Artist in this camp knows what it feels like to be part of the circle, to feel it break, and to help restore it. That isn't just theater training — it's something they carry long after the show closes.

The circle is alive. It begins again.
